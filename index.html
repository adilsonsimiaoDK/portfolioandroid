History of the Android Mascot

You probably know that the operating system Android, maintained by Google, is one of the most used for mobile devices worldwide. But perhaps you didn’t know that its friendly mascot has a name and a very curious story? So follow this article to learn a lot about it.

The first version

The first attempt to create a mascot emerged in 2007 and came from a developer named Dan Morrill. He says he opened Inkscape (free software for image vectorization) and created his own robot version. The goal was just to personify the system for his team; there was no company request to create a mascot.

This bizarre first version was even named in honor of its creator: they would be the Dandroids.

A new mascot emerges

The idea of having a mascot matured, and the task was handed over to a professional in the field. Russian illustrator Irina Blok, also a Google employee, took on the mission of representing the little robot in a more pleasant way.

The main idea of Irina Blok was to represent everything graphically with few strokes and in a flatter style. The design also needed to generate quick identification with whoever saw it. Thus, Bugdroid, the new Android mascot, was born.

The main inspiration for the new Bugdroid’s design came from those little figures that illustrate bathroom doors to indicate gender. Legend has it that the artist was creating at her desk in Google’s office, looked toward the bathrooms, and the identification was immediate: simple, clean, objective.

Want to learn more?

Another curious fact about Android is that each version has always been named after a dessert, following alphabetical order from version 1.5 to 9.0.

  • 1.5 - Cupcake
  • 1.6 - Donut
  • 2.0 - Eclair
  • 2.2 - Froyo
  • 2.3 - Gingerbread
  • 3.0 - Honeycomb
  • 4.0 - Ice Cream Sandwich
  • 4.1 - Jelly Bean
  • 4.4 - KitKat
  • 5.0 - Lollipop
  • 6.0 - Marshmallow
  • 7.0 - Nougat
  • 8.0 - Oreo
  • 9.0 - Pie

Unfortunately, Android Q didn’t exist, as Google decided to end this fun practice.
